# Rift Ascendant World Lore: Before the Gates

## Source Authority

This chapter is written from Rift Ascendant app canon. It synthesizes the local campaign, pantheon, sandbox, and handout material into a sourcebook-ready lore narrative for Wardens and players. It treats the app/codebase as the authoritative canon source.

Primary canon threads incorporated here include the Absolute, the Eternals and Exarchs, Kael Voss and the Reset, the System as containment protocol, the First Gate Surge, the rise of Ascendants, the Bureau age, Regent-class entities, the erased S-Rank of Yongsan, and the Yongsan Gate Surge.

---

## The World Before the Gates

Before the Gates, the world believed itself ordinary.

Cities rose by economics, not mana. Nations argued over borders, markets, trade routes, elections, industry, and ideology. People rode trains to work, studied for exams, cared for aging parents, opened cafés, fell in love, married in municipal chapels, and died without ever seeing the inside of another dimension. History books described wars, dynasties, pandemics, space programs, markets, and revolutions. They did not describe Anomalies.

That was the lie everyone lived inside.

The truth was older than the world’s memory.

Reality had already broken once.

Long before the current age, before the public knew the word Gate, before the Bureau filed its first incident report, before any Ascendant Field App displayed a Rank or Job, the cosmos had been shaped by a creator-force known in classified Bureau theology as the Absolute. The Absolute was not merely a god. It was the architect of the deep lattice: the hidden structure beneath time, mana, space, memory, death, and dimensional law.

From the Absolute came two great orders of beings.

The Eternals embodied enduring laws of existence: dominion, trial, memory, evolution, time, instinct, beauty, stone, frost, and final destruction. They were not simply worshipped powers. They were rules given will.

The Exarchs embodied radiant authority, preservation, celestial hierarchy, and defense against collapse. Solara, the Brightest Fragment, remains the clearest surviving echo of this order.

The Absolute also created the Dimensional Rifts.

They were not originally dungeons. They were pressure valves. The universe was never stable by nature. Mana, Void pressure, divine residue, dead timelines, and impossible causality accumulated between worlds. Without controlled release, the mortal plane would eventually rupture. The Rifts vented that pressure into bounded spaces.

The mortal world was not meant to understand them.

It was meant to survive them.

## The War Before Time

The first great age ended in war.

The Eternals and Exarchs, both born from the Absolute, came into conflict over the shape of reality. The Exarchs sought preservation, hierarchy, and radiant order. The Eternals embodied trial, instinct, destruction, evolution, memory, beauty, and change. Neither side was wholly good. Neither side was wholly evil. Both were necessary. Both were dangerous.

The war was fought in places mortal language cannot fully hold:

- **The Dimensional Gap**: the space between Gates and worlds.
- **The Celestial Bastion**: Solara’s realm of radiant defense.
- **The Dragon’s Maw**: Marthos’s realm of final deletion.
- **The Great Archive**: Xylo’s sanctuary of preserved eras.
- **The Origin Sanctum**: the guarded root of time watched by Kronos.
- **The Umbral Realm**: Kael Voss’s dominion, where death can become command rather than ending.

In this war, the Absolute died, broke, vanished, or became inaccessible to the current order of reality. No surviving canon source presents the event as clean. What matters is the consequence: the maker of the lattice was gone.

The world did not become free.

It became unstable.

The Void pressed inward. Timelines buckled. Some worlds were consumed outright. Others were rewritten. Countless possible histories became ash in the space between seconds.

Only a few powers remembered.

Kronos remembered.

Xylo recorded what could be saved.

And somewhere in the wreckage of a prior timeline, an Ascendant named Kael Voss kept climbing.

## Kael Voss and the Reset

Kael Voss began as something almost laughably small beside the beings he would later stand among: an E-Rank Ascendant who could barely survive a Gate.

In the previous timeline, Gates, Jobs, Ranks, and the System already existed in some form. Kael clawed his way through every dungeon, every Rank, every impossible trial. Where other Ascendants plateaued, he kept growing. He reached the Zenith. Then he surpassed it.

At the end of that doomed age, Kael Voss found or seized the Catalyst of Eras, the artifact of time now guarded by Kronos.

With it, he performed the impossible.

He reset the timeline.

This was not a gentle correction. It was cosmic surgery. The dead were not simply restored. The world was not simply rewound. Kael rewrote reality’s damaged sequence, sealing pieces of the prior age behind memory, myth, divine instinct, and hidden Gate architecture.

After the Reset, Kael returned as something more than an Ascendant.

He became the Weaver of the Absolute.

Not the original Absolute. Not merely its servant. Not its replacement in full.

A survivor wearing enough of the Absolute’s authority to hold the broken lattice together.

Kael no longer followed the Rift’s rules.

In the language of the deepest canon: he is the Rift.

## The System Beneath the World

After the Reset, the mortal world experienced a long false peace.

Humanity believed there had never been Gates. Governments did not publicly regulate Ascendants. Guilds did not yet dominate black markets. The Ascendant Bureau, at least in its modern form, did not exist.

But the lattice remained.

The System was woven over reality like a net. Publicly, the System would later appear as a beneficent interface: a power that granted Jobs, Paths, Powers, techniques, spells, Favor, and growth. Secretly, it was more complicated.

The System was a containment protocol.

It existed to:

- Detect dimensional pressure before collapse.
- Identify mortals with compatible mana circuits.
- Awaken those mortals into Ascendants.
- Classify their capabilities through Jobs, Paths, and Ranks.
- Drive Ascendants into Gates.
- Convert Gate-clearing activity into stabilizing energy.
- Prevent uncontrolled dimensional collapse.

The System protects humanity from collapse, but it also exploits humanity to maintain a cosmic machine humanity never chose to serve.

Valerius would later call it the Leash.

He was not entirely wrong.

## The Sleeping Mana World

Before the first public Gates, mana existed in hidden forms.

It appeared as coincidence, miracle, instinct, and taboo. Some families had impossible reflexes. Some children dreamed of places they had never seen. Some martial artists struck with force beyond physics. Some musicians moved crowds in ways no psychology could explain. Some priests healed the dying and called it faith. Some engineers built devices that should not have worked.

Most of these people never Awakened.

Their circuits were dormant.

Across the world, ancient places sat unknowingly atop buried mana structures:

- Pre-Awakening temples with sigil-stones beneath their foundations.
- Deep urban tunnels where resonance gathered in concrete and steel.
- Forgotten shrines where the names of Eternals survived as folklore.
- Natural caverns where Gate-crystal grew before any Gate opened.
- Mana veins beneath financial towers, hospitals, train depots, and old districts.
- Archive fragments recording histories no current timeline remembered.

The world before the Rifts was not empty of magic.

It was full of sealed doors.

## The First Gate Surge

Canon preserves the beginning of the Gate age as the First Gate Surge rather than as one single universally named portal. The safest historical framing is that the first public catastrophe involved multiple Gates, later simplified by civilians, governments, and survivors into different local myths.

The first Gates did not appear like a planned invasion.

They appeared like symptoms.

A subway wall shimmered blue and began breathing cold air. A rural hospital basement filled with impossible flowers. A bank vault opened into a corridor of ash. A school mirror showed a hallway that did not exist. A city park grew trees that had never evolved on Earth. A coastal road flooded with water from an ocean under alien stars.

At first, responders thought each incident was local:

- Gas leak.
- Terror attack.
- Earthquake.
- Hallucination.
- Military experiment.
- Religious event.
- Mass casualty anomaly.

Then the Anomalies came through.

Some were beasts. Some were intelligent. Some were fragments of ecosystems. Some were shaped like grief, hunger, memory, or law. Humanity learned the word Anomaly because anomaly was too small and alien was too inaccurate.

The Gates were not all the same. Some were stable portals. Some were pocket-embedded mirrors. Some were full dungeons. Some were sealed domains waiting to mature. Some were only cracks, leaking mana sickness into streets and hospitals.

The world nearly ended in those first days.

It did not because Solara intervened.

When the first gates tore open across the world, Solara stabilized the mana-flow and prevented the Gates from merging into a single planetary rupture. Without Solara’s Exarch authority, the first Gates would not have remained separate crises. They would have become one consuming wound.

The First Gate Surge created the modern world.

Not by destroying civilization, but by forcing civilization to admit reality was larger than itself.

## The First Awakened

The System responded to the Gates by selecting humans.

Not the most moral. Not the most prepared. Not always the strongest.

It selected those whose mana circuits could survive contact.

The first Awakenings were terrifying. People saw interfaces no one else could see. They heard chimes in their skulls. Their bones crystallized with mana. Their shadows moved before they did. Their voices carried resonance. Their hands produced flame, force, radiant light, umbral thread, or impossible geometry. Some collapsed. Some died. Some woke changed.

These Awakened became known as Ascendants.

The early Ascendants had no academies, no standardized Ranks, no safe onboarding, no Field App, and no clear legal status. They improvised. A nurse who could seal wounds became a field medic. A boxer who punched through Gate-crystal became a Destroyer prototype. A singer whose voice steadied panic became an Idol before the term existed. A scholar who saw equations in the air became a Mage. A mourner who returned from clinical death with a second shadow became a Revenant.

Only later did the System’s classifications become stable enough to name:

- **Jobs**: Awakened identity and combat mandate.
- **Paths**: specialization within a Job’s fate-pattern.
- **Powers, Spells, and Techniques**: usable expressions of mana.
- **Ranks**: measured threat, capability, and legal clearance.
- **Rift Favor**: the strange grace by which the lattice bends for those inside the crucible.

The first Ascendants were not heroes because they were noble.

They were heroes because they were the only ones who could walk into the Gates and come back.

Some did not come back.

Some came back wrong.

Some stayed.

## The Erased S-Rank of Yongsan

One of the oldest and most important local stories begins roughly fifty years before the Yongsan Gate Surge.

The modern Bureau claims it did not yet exist in its current form. Yet photographs, chapel records, and redacted family artifacts suggest there were predecessor agencies, early response divisions, or Bureau-like offices already forming around the first Awakened. This contradiction is canon-compatible: the Bureau has redacted its own origins many times.

In June 1978, a young woman named Park Jeong-hye married an Ascendant in dress uniform.

The groom’s name has been erased.

Not censored.

Erased.

His face cannot remain on photographs. His name does not register as sound. Records wear thin where he should appear. Memory itself rejects him.

He was an S-Rank before Ranks were fully understood. His power exceeded every measurement available at the time. Instead of merely clearing Gates, he entered one and stayed.

Ms. Park remembers him as a husband who promised to come home for lunch.

The Gate remembers him as something else.

Over decades inside a Gate dimension, he absorbed mana without limit. The dimension bent around him. His identity dissolved into domain authority. He did not die in the Gate.

He became the Gate.

He became the Regent of Yongsan.

This is the tragedy at the heart of the Yongsan campaign: the Regent is not invading because it hates the city. It is trying to come home to the neighborhood where it once lived, to the wife who waited, to the life the lattice erased.

That does not make it safe.

A homesick Regent can still destroy a city.

## The Birth of the Bureau Age

As Gates multiplied, governments failed to respond consistently. Police could cordon streets but not clear dungeons. Militaries could bombard Gate-adjacent zones but could not enter pocket dimensions safely. Hospitals could treat wounds but not mana rot. Universities could study residue but not survive field exposure.

The world needed a new institution.

Different countries formed early associations, strike teams, registries, and containment offices. Over time, these efforts consolidated into the global regulatory framework known as the Ascendant Bureau, or in some public documents, the Bureau of Ascendant Affairs.

Its global authority centered on Neo-Seoul.

The Bureau’s duties became:

- Register all Ascendants.
- Assess Rank.
- License Gate access.
- Regulate Relics, Runes, Sigils, and contraband.
- Contain Gate Breaks.
- Study Anomalies.
- Suppress dangerous knowledge.
- Maintain public order.
- Keep the System’s deeper truth hidden.

The Bureau saved millions.

It also became exactly what frightened people feared: a state-backed monopoly on survival.

Every Gate produced Essence, cores, Relics, Runes, and data. Every Ascendant became both a person and a strategic resource. Every unregistered clear became a crime. Every black-market Rune became a threat to public safety. Every corpse inside a Gate became an actuarial entry.

And every secret the Bureau could not explain became classified.

## Guilds, Corporations, and Black Markets

The Bureau was not alone for long.

Where there are Gates, there is wealth.

Where there is wealth, there are guilds.

Independent Ascendant guilds rose in the cracks between law and survival. Some were mercenary companies. Some were unions. Some were criminal networks. Some were community defense groups that became too useful to outlaw. The Vermillion Guild represents this tradition in Yongsan: faster than the Bureau, less regulated, more willing to trade in restricted Runes and contraband, and often more trusted by those abandoned during crisis.

Corporations followed.

Ascendant Corp became the symbol of private exploitation: Relic refinement, Essence technology, weaponized Gate research, private security contracts, debt-based Ascendant labor, and classified experiments that blurred the line between innovation and atrocity.

Academic institutions adapted as well. The National Aetheric Research Institute and similar bodies studied rift biology, Regent-class entities, mana exposure, and Gate ecology. Their best research was often classified before it could help the public.

The modern world did not become post-apocalyptic.

It became regulated, monetized, militarized, and haunted.

People still went to work.

The train still ran.

But every station had Gate alarms.

## The Pantheon of the Rift Age

To ordinary civilians, the Eternals are myth, cult language, or Ascendant superstition. To veteran Ascendants, they are patterns that keep recurring in Gates. To Bureau archivists, they are dangerous theological classifications. To Wardens, they are canon forces.

### Kael Voss, the Weaver of the Absolute

Kael is the Prime Eternal, the Umbral Eternal, the survivor of the prior timeline, and the one who used the Catalyst of Eras to enact the Reset. He commands Resurge: the power to raise fallen enemies as immortal Umbral Echoes. He is creation through dominion, death through command, and the living contradiction at the center of the Rift.

### Xylo, the Archivist of Eternity

Xylo remembers what reality tries to forget. He records Gates cleared, Ranks achieved, Ascendants lost, and truths that timelines bury. Bureau researchers and scholars unknowingly serve his pattern whenever they preserve forbidden records.

### Solara, the Brightest Fragment

Solara is the last major echo of the Radiant Exarchs. She stabilized the first Gates and prevented global annihilation. Holy Knights, guardians, healers, and protectors draw near her authority when they defend life against Void collapse.

### Marthos, the Dragon-King of Void

Marthos is deletion, entropy, and necessary destruction. He is not simply evil. He is what comes when a realm is too corrupted to save. Memory-eating Anomalies, void-breath, and final erasure bear his signature.

### Vaelen, the Eternal of White Flame

Vaelen is martial trial and combat supremacy. His Cataclysm Gates test those who seek Zenith-class mastery. The Bureau’s harshest certification trials echo his doctrine.

### Lyra, the Queen of the Swarm

Lyra governs Gate ecology: mutant flora, impossible predators, hive organisms, and overnight evolution. Summoners and gate ecologists fear and revere her.

### Kronos, the Fragment of the Absolute

Kronos anchors the timeline. He remembers the world before the Reset: the Eternals and Exarchs, the death of the Absolute, and Kael’s use of the Catalyst. Without Kronos, time would collapse back toward the Void.

### Sylph, the Eternal of Transfiguration

Sylph governs Rifts, portals, wind-mana, and spatial movement. Bureau Containment quietly suspects she influences where Gate Breaks occur.

### Nyx, the Eternal of Fangs

Nyx is instinct, ambush, predation, and survival beneath civilization’s mask. She reminds Ascendants that Rank and interface do not remove the oldest law: predator and prey.

### Elara, the Aesthetic Harmony

Elara preserves beauty, art, inspiration, and soulcraft. In horror, her gifts are often inverted: a wedding photo, a song, a memory, or a flower made wrong.

### Golem, the Eternal of Giants

Golem is endurance, foundation, containment, and the refusal to fall. Every wall that holds against a Gate Break echoes his presence.

### Cipher, the Eternal of Frost

Cipher is cold judgment, hidden truth, stillness, and preserved memory. Mystery Gates and mental trials often carry his frost.

## Gate Breaks and the Cost of Delay

As the decades passed, humanity learned the difference between a Gate and a Gate Break.

A Gate in its Blue Phase is a warning.

A Gate Break is a failure.

When a Gate is not cleared, it ruptures. The dungeon stops being contained. Anomalies pour into the real world. Terrain can overwrite city blocks. Mana storms poison civilians. Gate ecosystems begin reproducing in Earth’s atmosphere.

The Bureau’s strictness was born from these failures.

### The Seoul Gate Breach and Seoul Cascade, 2019

A Class-S or Regent-class crisis erupted during rush hour. Old Man Crane, then known as White Heron, faced the first Regent he personally encountered. Casualties included 147 Ascendants and 2,300 civilians.

Bureau hesitation made the disaster worse. Jurisdictional conflict cost hours. Guildmaster Orin, then Bureau, disobeyed orders and saved forty-one civilians. He was punished. This failure later shaped the Vermillion Guild’s philosophy: survive first, obey later.

The Seoul Cascade became the Bureau’s shame and the Guilds’ founding argument.

### São Paulo, 2021

A second Regent-class event ended differently. A strike team led by White Heron sealed the Regent rather than destroying it. The Gate closed permanently, but the entity may still exist within, sleeping.

This event matters because it proves Regents are not merely anomalies to be slain.

They can be sealed.

Perhaps even understood.

But the cost is terrible.

## Regents: The Dungeons That Dream

Most Anomalies are born from Gates.

Regents create Gates.

A Regent is not merely a boss inside a dungeon. A Regent is a domain-consciousness: a being powerful enough to generate its own dimensional reality. Its Relics are fragments of its power scattered across dimensional barriers, not treasure in the ordinary sense. They are anchors.

To face a Regent is to enter a mind large enough to become architecture.

The Yongsan Regent is unusual even by Regent standards because its mana signature carries grief, homesickness, memory loss, and human attachment. Dr. Hayashi believes communication may be possible. Old Man Crane notes that this Regent feels less hungry than previous ones.

It feels lost.

That difference does not lower the threat.

It deepens it.

## Subject Zero Beneath Yongsan

Beneath the Yongsan Mana Vein Network lies a secret older than the current Surge.

Bureau Central Command has used the district’s mana veins as a containment buffer for a dormant S-Rank Anomaly known only as Subject Zero. It is not the Regent. It is older. Its files are nameless. Its containment has degraded as the Yongsan Surge worsens.

This means Yongsan is not merely unlucky.

It is structurally compromised.

The Regent’s homecoming, the Awoko Cult’s rituals, Blackwood’s classified mission, the Mana Vein sensors, the Bureau’s redactions, and Subject Zero’s containment are all pressure points in the same damaged lattice.

The city is not just under attack.

It is built on a sleeping wound.

## The Road to Yongsan

In the current era, the public believes the Gate system is understood.

This is false.

The Bureau can measure Ranks, but not destiny. It can classify Anomalies, but not explain why grief becomes a anomaly. It can license Ascendants, but not tell them why the System chose them. It can confiscate Runes, but not stop the Eternals from leaving fingerprints inside every Gate.

Then Yongsan begins to pulse.

Thirty-six hours before the district quarantine, an E-Rank Gate signature appears inside the Hyeonsan Memory Lodge, a dementia ward in a shuttered hospital wing. No visible portal is found. The Gate is inside a mirror.

The Anomaly within is called The Diagnosed.

It is made of memory, dementia, Marthos-aspect void, Cipher-aspected frost, and the pre-Surge resonance of the Regent trying to come home. It whispers the same demand:

> Let me come home.

The party’s first horror is not a battlefield.

It is a room full of old photographs where a wife still waits for a husband erased from reality.

Twelve hours later, Yongsan erupts.

Nineteen Gates appear in a single district.

The Bureau declares a Restricted Zone.

The Ascendants are called in.

The Regent’s Domain begins its fourteen-day Blue Phase.

The city clock starts.

## Public Truth, Restricted Truth, Warden Truth

### What Civilians Know

- Gates are dangerous portals to monster-filled pocket dimensions.
- Ascendants clear Gates for money, duty, fame, or survival.
- The Bureau regulates Ascendants to keep civilians safe.
- Guilds are risky but sometimes more practical than the Bureau.
- S-Rank Gates are national disasters.
- Regents are world-ending entities.

### What Registered Ascendants Know

- Gates have Ranks and timers.
- Blue Phase means the Gate is open but contained.
- Red Phase means Gate Break.
- Anomaly cores, Runes, Sigils, Relics, and Essence have real value.
- The Bureau hides things.
- The System gives power, but never explains itself.
- Re-Awakening is possible but rare and dangerous.
- Regent Relics can weaken a Regent-class entity.

### What Bureau Command Knows

- The System is older than public science can explain.
- Gate behavior follows patterns tied to Eternals, Exarchs, and the deep lattice.
- Some records redact themselves.
- Some names cannot be preserved.
- Some crises are allowed to unfold because the alternative may be worse.
- Yongsan’s mana veins are part of an older containment structure.

### What Wardens Know

- The System is a containment net built from Absolute architecture.
- The Absolute is dead, fragmented, or parasitically persistent depending on which evidence is trusted.
- Kael Voss reset the timeline using the Catalyst of Eras.
- Kronos remembers the prior world.
- The first Gates were pressure-valve failures, not random portals.
- Solara prevented the first Gates from merging into global annihilation.
- The Yongsan Regent was once a human S-Rank Ascendant.
- The Regent is not trying to conquer.
- It is trying to come home.
- Stopping it may still be necessary.
- Killing it may not be the only ending.
- Subject Zero is still sleeping.

## Campaign Tone

Rift Ascendant is modern urban fantasy with dungeon-crawl bones, cosmic horror veins, and manhwa escalation.

A proper Warden tone should emphasize the ordinary beside the impossible: convenience stores under Gate light, subway maps marked with dungeon entrances, Bureau alerts on smartphones, and coffee shops beside mana cordons.

It should also emphasize power with bureaucracy: licenses, Rank assessments, legal Gate access, Bureau armories, restricted Rune lists, corporate Relic patents, Guild bounties, and black-market Essence.

Most importantly, it should reveal cosmic truth through personal grief: a dead strike team, a wife waiting fifty years, a child drawing the final battle, an old S-Rank drinking tea before his last raid.

The System is both gift and leash.

Regents are tragedy, not just bosses.

And every Gate is a door that asks what a person is willing to become in order to come back out.

## Opening Read-Aloud

Before the Gates, the world believed itself alone.

It was not.

Beneath every city street, under every temple foundation, behind every mirror that reflected a face too long, the lattice waited. It had been woven before history, before memory, before the current shape of time. The Absolute built it. The Eternals and Exarchs fought over it. Kael Voss rewrote it. Kronos remembered it. Xylo recorded it. Solara held it shut when it first began to tear.

Humanity called the first tears disasters.

The System called them pressure valves.

The Bureau called them Gates.

The things that came through were named Anomalies because no older word survived contact with them.

Then humans began to Awaken.

A nurse sealed wounds with light. A boxer cracked Gate-stone with his bare hands. A student saw equations burning over a anomaly’s heart. A singer calmed a shelter with one shaking note. A widower stepped into a dungeon and came back with someone else’s shadow.

The System gave them Jobs. The Bureau gave them licenses. The world gave them impossible expectations.

They became Ascendants.

For decades, they held the line.

They cleared Gates, harvested cores, buried friends, signed waivers, broke laws, joined Guilds, paid Essence taxes, and pretended the System was on their side because the alternative was too terrifying to say aloud.

But the oldest Gates do not forget.

Fifty years ago, before the Bureau became what it is now, before Gates were understood, an S-Rank Ascendant entered a subway Gate and never returned. His name vanished from records. His face wore away from photographs. His wife remembered only that he had promised to come home for lunch.

Now Yongsan is glowing violet.

Nineteen Gates have opened in a single district.

A Regent is waking beneath the city.

And somewhere inside the highest Gate, something that used to be human is still trying to come home.
